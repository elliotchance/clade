atmosphere [edit] [view history]
The atmosphere of music refers to the familiar sensations and images which are conveyed to the listener. Atmosphere is distinguished from mood in that the latter is largely associated with emotions, not imagery.

apocalyptic [edit] [view history]
Music which evokes imagery of the end of the world or catastrophic destruction.

bright [edit] [view history]
Music with a bright atmosphere evokes imagery full of light or liveliness. Brightness that is imagined as full of light can come from various sources of light found in reality, such as fire, heat, or luminescent objects. Brightness that is imagined as full of life refers to a sensation of vividness that typically bears some relation to the perceived mood of the music.

Brightness in music can also refer to the sound quality of an instrument or voice. Brightness in this sense refers to a clear and strong high-frequency sound. Certain instruments, such as the trumpet, naturally produce bright timbres, but the modes utilized within music can help reinforce a bright sound; generally speaking, in Western music theory, major scales carry a brighter sound, whereas minor scales are the opposite, bearing darker sonic qualities. The tonal quality of an instrument also depends on the recording and/or production of the music being played. A bright atmosphere can be brought about by any of these definitions, if not all at once.

An ominous atmosphere evokes a sense that something evil, unfortunate, or unpleasant will happen. It is typically perceived as an outcome that cannot be avoided or as a warning must be heeded at all costs; either way, such a sensation consequently makes the listener feel powerless in their situation. Because believing in an omen can be superstitious, there is sometimes a supernatural aura surrounding an ominous atmosphere.

form [edit] [view history]
A musical form describes an overall structure of a music work. More specifically, a form may define the order and type of various sections within a song, the instruments that are used, and/or other aspects of music composition.

ballad [edit] [view history]
The term "ballad" originally refers to songs adapted from narrative poems and composed in short stanzas. With the emergence of popular music, the term also began to be used for slow and usually sentimental love songs.

concept album [edit] [view history]
Concept albums are composed of songs focused on a unified common theme or idea, achieved through interlinked narrative structure and/or cohesive musical composition. They are notably unique from standard albums, which are generally not focused on a singular theme, or are - but which do not utilise a sense of connection between the overall songs. Concept albums are not always lyrical, as instrumental albums can still create a connected sense of an individual idea through various compositional techniques.

Though originally having its roots in religious chants (being Italian for "in the manner of the chapel"), it later became synonymous with the phrase "unaccompanied vocal music". Despite this, some a cappella music does feature minor or light instrumentation, usually in the form of percussive elements.

A musical parody takes elements from established artists, styles, or individual songs and restructures their content lyrically, musically, or by satirizing their intent to create an intentionally humorous or ironic adaptation of the original work(s).

pastiche [edit] [view history]
A pastiche is a work deliberately written or performed in the style of another artist, composer, genre, school or period. Pastiche is distinguished from pasticcio (Italian for pie), a type of medley of arias from different operas that emerged in Italy in the late-17th century, as well as from parody, which involves the intent to criticise, satirise or ridicule. This intent is often absent in pastiche, which can be neutral or even affectionate towards its source material.

stream of consciousness [edit] [view history]
Stream of consciousness refers to a narrative mode that mimics the rapid flow of thoughts running through the narrator's head. The term was coined in The Principles of Psychology (1890) by psychologist William James to describe the uninterrupted "stream" of thought, but it began to be used to describe a literary technique from the late 1910s onward, notably embraced by modernist-era writers such as James Joyce and Virginia Woolf as well as the Beat Generation.

Stream-of-consciousness narratives are typically characterized by nonlinear storytelling, emphasis on sensory perception, occasional repetition of phrases, and an instantaneous "interior monologue" of the narrator's thoughts. In a musical context, this mode can appear in a variety of inflections, themes, and genres of music, but the core tenets of the style stay the same.

style [edit] [view history]
A musical style describes how one or more aspects of music, such as melody, harmony, rhythm, timbre, tempo, dynamics, and form, are used. The word "style" is sometimes used interchangeably with "genre", but it is much more specific and detailed, denoting particular characteristics of music rather than the sound as a whole.

angular [edit] [view history]
Angularity in music refers to a particular form of melody where there are large jumps in intervals and sudden shifts in melodic direction, leading to a sense of dissonance and tension due to the resulting jagged textures. Angularity draws its name from its distinct sound, which evokes imagery of sharp angles and zigzagging lines. This form of playing is frequently accompanied by "start-and-stop" rhythms, where the instruments begin and end at irregular moments. Interplay between different instruments is also common, with angular countermelodies often being described as "interlocking." This form of playing is particularly common in Post-Punk, New Wave, and Math Rock, where it is used to create a mechanical, jagged, or uneasy sound.

anthemic [edit] [view history]
Anthemic music is made suitably for anthems, with notable characteristics such as containing an easy-to-remember musical theme, utilising call-and-response lyrics, being repetitive, and having a powerful sound.

atmospheric [edit] [view history]
Atmospheric music puts special emphasis on the timbre and texture of the music, its "atmosphere". The atmosphere in such music becomes the core of the composition as opposed to the more melodic, rhythmic or technical forms of music.

atonal [edit] [view history]
Atonal music can be generally seen as music which lacks focus on a key. Commonly, it is used to describe songs in which notes on a chromatic scale are played independently of one another. The roots of atonal music can be traced to late 19th to early 20th century Classical Music, where composers would seemingly forego the teachings and fundamentals of composition set out during the common practice period (c. 1600-1900). It is also not limited to just forms of Experimental music, but it is found more commonly in leftfield genres due to its mostly criticized practice.

avant-garde [edit] [view history]
Avant-garde music deliberately alienates or challenges audiences while pushing the boundaries of what may be acceptable in the cultural realm. Its music is often Experimental, radical, or unorthodox, and may exaggerate methods of recording, composition, or execution to hyperbole. Some quintessential examples are Erik Satie's Vexations (instructs the player to perform a theme 840 times) or John Cage's 4'33" (a three-movement piece consisting of silence). Avant-garde sensibilities can also be found in non-classical work, like that by Sun Ra, The Residents, and Captain Beefheart. Because the avant-garde challenges the status quo in favour of unique or original ideas, it is considered to be at the forefront of experimentation or innovation in its field.

bassy [edit] [view history]
Bassy music focuses on frequencies under 200 Hz, usually performed on a bass instrument such as a contrabass, electric bass, or synth bass, or featuring low percussion such as a kick drum. Frequencies under 80 Hz are referred to as sub-bass and are often emphasized in Dance and Hip Hop music. In the vinyl era, bass frequencies were limited by the width of the groove, but the introduction of the compact cassette and the 12-inch vinyl single allowed for greater bass frequency response. Simultaneously, subwoofers capable of reproducing sub-bass were introduced both in discotheques and Jamaican sound systems, leading to bassy music like Dub and Disco, which had a major impact on subsequent producers.

A buzzy style makes use of a continuous low, vibrating sound, similar to that of a bee. Music in this style often adopts certain production choices and techniques, such as the use of synths, as well as distorted percussion. This style can sometimes be perceived as exaggerated or even intense.

complex [edit] [view history]
Complex music comprises many elements interconnected in complicated ways, utilizing various changes in any of its aspects, particularly tempo, rhythm, melody, dynamics, and structure.

crackly [edit] [view history]
Crackly music uses dry, sharp, unpitched "crackling" sounds in its production. These are often sampled from analog media such as vinyl or tape, but they may also include natural sounds such as fire, acoustic effects such as guitar fret noise or string buzz, voice cracks, and digital effects with similar timbres. These elements may be part of the original recording in lo-fi productions, or they may be added to a higher-fidelity mix as is common in Trip Hop and many styles of Hip Hop.

dense [edit] [view history]
Dense music is characterised by an intensive, busy, thick, and often multi-layered sound.

dissonant [edit] [view history]
Dissonant music is characterised by a use of harsh and/or inharmonious sound.

eclectic [edit] [view history]
Eclectic music combines different techniques, styles, aesthetics, instrumentation, and genres through the duration of a song or entire release.

fuzzy [edit] [view history]
Fuzz is characterized by a distorted, heavily saturated sound, changing the signal to a square wave and producing a long-lasting sustain and an occasionally lo-fi sound. It is commonly applied to instruments like guitar and bass through the use of fuzz pedals as seen in Rock offshoots like Psychedelic Rock, Stoner Metal, and Shoegaze. It is also seen in synth-driven Electronic genres like Electropop and Electro House.

heavy [edit] [view history]
Heavy music uses techniques such as distorted and downtuned riffs, forceful rhythm section, loud low frequencies, and abrasive and aggressive performance to create a powerful and pounding sound. Metaphorically speaking, it describes music which feels to have a lot of crushing "weight".

lo-fi [edit] [view history]
Lo-fi (short for low fidelity) is a production style in which the recordings are made in a lower quality than was standard for the time in which they were made. This is often done deliberately. However, in other cases, it's simply a consequence of circumstances.

lush [edit] [view history]
Lush music uses techniques such as rich textures, layered instrumentation, extended chords, string arrangements, and clean production to create a tender and colourful sound. The Wall of Sound production technique is often utilised to create such a style.

maximalist [edit] [view history]
Maximalist music is characterized by a grandiose sound that embraces heterogeneity and excessive use of musical elements, favoring intense dynamics, lush arrangements, thick textures, and extensive instrumentation or studio layering.

melodic [edit] [view history]
Melodic music puts strong emphasis on melody compared with other aspects of composition such as rhythm, harmony, or texture. The most prominent feature of melodic music is the dominance of a strong melody line.

microtonal [edit] [view history]
Microtonal music can refer either to a tuning system consisting of intervals smaller than the semitone (the smallest interval in standard Western music, often called the "half-step") or, more generally, to any music that does not divide the octave into twelve equal parts.

minimalistic [edit] [view history]
Minimalistic music is characterised by a stripped-down sound which keeps only essential sounds, uses predominantly conventional song structures, and narrow instrumentation.

monophonic [edit] [view history]
Monophony is considered the simplest musical texture and found in every culture, consisting of a single melody which is not accompanied by harmony or unfixed chords. This is often done with a single voice or instrument. It can also be performed by choirs and ensembles, where the melody is performed in unison, or with performers playing at fixed intervals to account for natural differences in pitch. If monophony is paired with a drone note, it is known as biphony, and if different performers play the same melody with different rhythms or embellishments, it is called heterophony. Monophony can be accompanied by non-melodic percussion.

muffled [edit] [view history]
Muffled music typically contains dampened high frequencies, focusing on low and midrange frequencies to create a distant, indistinct, or subdued sound. A muffled sound may result from intentional production techniques such as low-pass filters, reverb, and downsampling, poor recording equipment, physical obstructions between the microphone and sound source, layers of noise that drown out other elements, or the use of lossy audio codecs, such as MP3, in lobit music.

noisy [edit] [view history]
Noisy music puts strong emphasis on distorted timbres and textures. This is usually achieved through loud playing, conglomerations of sounds, and/or the extreme application of effects such as echo or fuzz.

piercing [edit] [view history]
Piercing music uses very high-pitched, sharp-sounding frequencies. These are often achieved through extreme feedback or notes played well above the typical upper range but may also be caused by other means, such as very bright equalization or a high Q factor. In some cases, the purpose might be to represent the sound of tinnitus, such as in "Ventolin." Due to higher frequencies naturally sounding louder than lower ones at the same volume (an effect known as the Fletcher–Munson curve), some listeners may find these frequencies painful or irritating.

progressive [edit] [view history]
Progressive music innovates musical conventions through the use of established methods of composition and newer derivative forms and styles. It extensively uses techniques from music theory to create multistructured and/or structurally complex music. In the 20th century, this was initially achieved by using structural unity (formalism), style synthesis, and integration (eclecticism). Newer progressive music has since drawn from various cultural domains, such as Western Classical Music, Celtic Folk Music, South Asian Music, or African Music. Later in the 20th century, the approach led to the creation of Progressive Rock and Progressive Pop, which derived inspiration from techniques in Jazz and Classical Music. Progressive elements may also be found in various music styles and genres that are not explicitly preceded by the term progressive.

This is especially notable in House, Techno, and Breakbeat Hardcore (also known as Rave music). In other genres, such as Drone, the entire song itself can be focused on a monotonal and repeated note played throughout the full track. Other genres such as Krautrock and Minimalism are also notable for their frequent use of repetition.

composition [edit] [view history]
Musical composition refers to the means and methods used to compose a piece of music. Composition itself can occur at any point during a song's 'life', whether through planned writing and playing to a set of musical notation, or composed on-the-fly through improvisation. Using standard (and sometimes non-standard) musical notation, composers create pieces through the standard aspects of music: melody, harmony, form, pitch, dynamics, rhythm, timbre, order, texture, and tempo.

Aleatoric (or chance) music is said to be composed "if its course is determined in general but depends on chance in detail." call and response [edit] [view history]
Call and response is a compositional technique in which a "call" phrase is performed and then another phrase serves as a "response" to the call. The phrases can be vocal or instrumental as well as composed or improvised; they can be performed by groups or by individual vocalists or instrumentalists.

Call and response is prevalent across many cultures and genres. Sometimes derived from African traditions, its influence can be commonly seen in African American Blues and Gospel and also in many Latin American musical traditions, notably Cuban Music, where it is called coro-pregón.

free rhythm [edit] [view history]
Free rhythm is a technique in which a regular underlying pulse or pattern of beats (meter) is eschewed in favor of irregular, free-flowing rhythms. The use of free rhythm is rare in Western popular music but is commonly found in various kinds of Experimental music, atmosphere-focused genres, such as Ambient, and a variety of Regional Music styles across the globe.

The gallop is a rhythm created by playing an eighth note followed by two sixteenth notes. The rhythm is closely associated with Heavy Metal, with Iron Maiden most famously making use of the gallop.

generative music [edit] [view history]
Generative music, a term created by Brian Eno, describes music with a generated composition applied to a musical scale and array of instruments, which cannot be repeated by its creator. It applies to, for example, recordings of musicians performing generated pieces, music created and played digitally with custom algorithmic software, or organic beings/movements/cycles applied to music. Four perspectives are usually placed on generative music; structural (the composition is created from a musical 'grammar' based on a style), behavioral (no musical input), procedural (a procedure/idea is placed on the musical setting before the piece is initiated) and emergent (music created from organic interactions).

jamming [edit] [view history]
Originating among Jazz musicians playing together outside of gigs, jamming refers to a spontaneous form of improvisation in which musicians improvise in a more casual and social manner than is common in other improvisational forms. There is typically less enforced structure or limits on the improv, with jams being capable of being either incredibly prolonged or curt, meaning songs are generally created through improvisation, as opposed to the improv just being a section of a larger, more structured and arranged piece. The key component of jamming is its social aspect, with musicians playing off one another and collaborating on a jam live, as opposed to just emphasizing the position of a single, soloing musician. As can be expected from the name, Jam Band music makes extensive use of jamming techniques.

Through-composed form is an approach to musical structure that is continuous and nonrepetitive. While the term is mostly applied to vocal songs it can also be used to describe instrumental compositions.

uncommon time signatures [edit] [view history]
In music, meter refers to the regular pattern of strong and weak beats within a piece (often expressed notationally through a time signature). Most Western music can be sorted into the categories of duple meter, triple meter, and quadruple meter, which divide each measure into 2, 3, or 4 equal subdivisions, respectively. These include "simple" meters such as 2/2, 3/4, and 4/4, as well as "compound" meters with a triplet subdivision per beat, such as 6/8, 9/8 and 12/8.

Meters that do not fit into the above categories may be called "complex," "irregular," "uncommon," or "odd," though definitions of these terms sometimes vary. A complex meter often takes the form of a repeating pattern combining groups of 2 and 3; for example, 5/4 is often felt as a group of 3 beats followed by a group of 2 beats. Much more intricate patterns are also possible.

Complex meters are relatively uncommon in Western popular music but are used in a variety of other styles. Modern Classical composers such as Igor Stravinsky extensively used complex meters with frequent time signature changes in works like Le sacre du printemps [The Rite of Spring]. Complex meters are also commonplace in various technically oriented Rock and Metal subgenres, ranging from Progressive Rock to Technical Death Metal, as well as certain styles of Regional Music, such as Balkan Folk Music.

sampling [edit] [view history]
Sampling is a production technique in which an artist or band lifts a portion of a pre-existing sound recording and reuses it in a new piece. Samples can range from anything from milliseconds of audio (such as is found in Micromontage music), to complete songs being reused in new ways (such as in Plunderphonics). While most samples generally consist of a percussive pattern or vocal clip, samplers have used almost every conceivable piece of audio in order to create new music. Sampling dates as far back as the 1950s, where Musique concrète and Tape Music artists experimented with manipulation of tape loops. It further developed when Sound Collage began creating artistic compositions made (usually) entirely from previous sound material. Through the 1960s and 1970s, Experimental Rock and Punk bands began to use sampling in more commonplace music. Industrial music brought the use of home-made sampling devices, and soon after commercial samplers became more publicly available. The use of sampling in more popular music was further expanded on with the rise of Hip Hop in the late 1970s and 1980s. Artists began more commonly lifting samples of previous works into new compositions, and Electronic music saw more prominent uses of the technique. Today, sampling can be heard in almost every genre of music, as sampling equipment and digital software makes it possible for any artist to use it in their music.

Vocal chops are cleanly cut and usually very short vocal samples that are generally rearranged or repeated to create a melody or rhythm distinct in sound from the original sample.

Wall of Sound [edit] [view history]
The Wall of Sound (or Spector Sound) is a production formula developed by producer-songwriter Phil Spector in the 1960s. It involves many acoustic/electric guitars, keyboards, and percussive instruments playing in unison. The resultant sound would then be mixed closely together and accentuated with reverb. The Wall of Sound was built from carefully-blended instrumental colors, as opposed to simply raising the levels of multiple instruments to the point of distortion. Use of layered channels of monaural sound, blended together in a carefully arranged way are core to the overall sound, and is essential to avoid a busy or overly dense sound. Other producers and artists, most notably Brian Wilson, have adopted the technique.

rubato [edit] [view history]
Tempo rubato (Italian for stolen time; often shortened to rubato) is a phrasing technique in which the tempo of a piece is sped up and slowed down at the performer's or conductor’s discretion, while still retaining the same pace overall. This ebbing and flowing of time creates a sound that is both more natural and more expressive than a straight tempo performance, which may be seen as lacking nuance or emotion in comparison. Rubato is often associated with Romanticism, especially the works of Frédéric Chopin, but is also used in certain strains of Jazz, as well as the traditional music of various regions (with Polish Folk Music, for instance, having influenced Chopin's use of rubato).

theme [edit] [view history]
A theme is a prominent or recurring topic, subject, idea, or message of the music. Themes range from concepts that the work is about to statements that it makes about them. Themes are commonly expressed through the lyrics, but certain sounds, styles, works, and imagery can be used to reference associated places, cultures, events, periods, etc.

In interpretation and art studies, the theme of the work is often understood more narrowly as the underlying message or insight communicated through the subject matter. It can be divided into two parts: thematic concept and thematic statement. Thematic concept refers to ideas and aspects of the human condition that are explored in the work, while thematic statement implicitly or explicitly conveys the artist's perspective on them. In broader, colloquial usage, the subject of the work in general — that is, the things that it is "about" — is also referred to as the theme.

gore [edit] [view history]
Gore-themed music focuses on extreme violence and bloodshed. It focuses on portraying gory imagery with over-the-top gruesome descriptions of violence against the human body, such as mutilation, decapitation, and decomposition, sometimes while incorporating the use of medical terminology and pathological diseases for increased shock value. Gory imagery is often attributed to Metal genres such as Death Metal, as well as Hip Hop artists within the Horrorcore genre, with many bands and artists in these style taking lyrical influences from Slasher films and Sadistic Horror. Death metal bands that pioneered this aesthetic also went on to inspire more extreme metal subgenres, such as Goregrind, in which gore-themed music became more exclusive to their respective genres.

farming [edit] [view history]
Farming is the cultivation of the soil. It encompasses a range of practices and traditions in the areas of planting, tending, and harvesting, as well as breeding and raising livestock. Farming has taken many forms throughout thousands of years of human history and prehistory, categorized into various forms of shifting cultivation, subsistence farming, and intensive farming. Being a central practice across many human societies, farming has long been a prominent theme in musical traditions up to the present, often in the form of work songs but also associated with other facets of rural life.

mining [edit] [view history]
Mining is the extraction of valuable resources from the surface of the earth, often using tools such as pickaxes and drill rigs. Songs about mining typically focus on the hardship, danger, and physical endurance required for the work.

Mining songs in the Folk and Work Song genres date back many centuries, with traditional Geordie song "The Collier's Rant" potentially composed around 1650. These songs were popular in mining communities around the UK, in areas such as Cornwall, Wales and Lancashire. Traditional mining songs were also present in South Africa ("Shosholoza"), Canada ("Jolly Wee Miner Men") and Australia ("The Miner").

In the late 20th century, mining songs became more prominent in mainstream music. Songs such as Tennessee Ernie Ford's "Sixteen Tons," Bee Gees's "New York Mining Disaster 1941," and Johnny Cash's version of "Dark as the Dungeon" on At Folsom Prison brought miners' experience to a wider audience. Contemporary Folk artists such as Pete Seeger and Woody Guthrie also wrote many songs about miners and mining disasters, tying them to broader themes about human resilience and labor rights.

With the boom in popularity of the game Minecraft in the early 2010s, a wave of parody songs about the topic of mining appeared, with tracks such as "Don't Mine at Night - Minecraft Parody" and "Mine the Diamond (A Minecraft Song)" addressing the subject, albeit in a fictional manner.

crooning [edit] [view history]
Crooning is a smooth, intimate vocal style that arose in 1920s Jazz and Traditional Pop as improved microphones allowed singers to explore greater dynamic ranges. This style was most popular from the 1940s to the late 1960s, with Bing Crosby and Frank Sinatra being some of the most well-known crooners.

falsetto [edit] [view history]
Falsetto is a vocal technique occupying frequency ranges above the register of modal voice (the vocal range most commonly used in speech and singing). Falsetto is produced by vibrating the ligamentous edges of the vocal chords. The falsetto register gives vocals a breathy, airy sound while being more limited in tone quality, although vocal training can produce a stronger falsetto sound. Falsetto is widely used across folk, classical, and popular music traditions.

The term was historically used to refer exclusively to male singers, and it was formerly believed that only men were capable of producing falsetto, although scientific research has since proven that women also have a falsetto register.

sing-rapping [edit] [view history]
Sing-rapping features rapped vocals with highly melodic elements. It is most commonly found in a Hip Hop context, with Kanye West's album 808s & Heartbreak helping to popularize the style. Rappers like Young Thug and Future have found mainstream success and influence while often using the technique.
Sing-rapping also exists outside of a Hip Hop context, albeit less commonly. Singers like Taylor Swift and Ariana Grande have seen mainstream success with the technique on Pop songs.

talk-singing [edit] [view history]
Talk-singing, or Sprechgesang, is a technique that incorporates speech-like vocals. Sprechgesang initially referred to the recitative technique found in Opera, but it later came to refer to a vocal technique used in popular music, such as Rock, which emphasizes speech-like sounds rather than melody in singing, similar to the Sprechstimme vocal technique associated with composers from the Second Viennese School, like Arnold Schoenberg. It has also been connected to the vocal technique found in Talking Blues and has drawn comparisons to rapping as well, though talk-singing significantly differs from the latter and does not share its emphasis on rhythm. This technique was first popularized by vocalists such as Lou Reed and Bob Dylan in the 1960s and '70s; it later spread to Post-Punk and Alternative Rock. In the 2010s and '20s, artists from the Windmill Scene also incorporated the technique.

Whispering is an unvoiced mode of phonation in which the vocal cords are abducted so that they do not vibrate, using the breath but not the voice.

yarling [edit] [view history]
Yarling is a deep, guttural vocal style with affected pronunciation, popularized by the Grunge band Pearl Jam's lead singer Eddie Vedder. Yarling came to be found in subsequent popular grunge and Post-Grunge bands like Stone Temple Pilots and Creed; however, the vocal style waned in popularity with the decline of the second wave of post-grunge.
