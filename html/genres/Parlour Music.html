Parlour Music

AKA: Parlor Music Salon Music • 141 releases
Early form of light, sentimental popular music; popular in USA and Europe from the late 1700s to the 1870s.
Read more Parlour music is considered the earliest form of commercial popular music. It was disseminated by sheet music in the pre-recorded music era, originally intended to be performed in the parlours of homes by amateur singers and pianists. Parlour music is typically designed to be sung with piano accompaniment, though other arrangements are possible as well. The lyrics of parlour songs are typically lighthearted and are often comedic or romantic in nature, though parlour songs could often be satirical as well.

Parlour songs were originally often simplified adaptations of songs that came from a variety of sources, such as Opera, Art Song, Minstrelsy, and Folk music. As more songs were composed specifically for use in the home, however, parlour music evolved into a genre of music in its own right. Notable composers of this style include George F. Root, Carrie Jacobs-Bond, and Stephen Foster. Stephen Foster in particular has achieved a lasting status in American music, with many of his songs, such as Beautiful Dreamer and Oh! Susanna, being well-known in the United States to this day.

Parlour music declined in popularity in the late 1800s as more options for seeing professional performances opened up to the middle class and more complex genres such as Ragtime emerged. However, parlour music was a crucial precursor to the later emergence of commercial Pop music, being an important influence on Tin Pan Alley in particular.
