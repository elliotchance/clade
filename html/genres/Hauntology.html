Hauntology

AKA: Memoradelia • 942 releases • 2 cataloged
Distorts musical styles and broadcast media from the mid-20th century post-war era using Ambient and Sound Collage techniques to create an uncanny, lost, "haunted" atmosphere.
Read more Hauntology, in reference to music, is a broad genre unified by its deliberately uncanny, lost, "haunted" sense of nostalgia. Hauntology artists focus their sense of nostalgia on mid-20th century post-war broadcast media (recorded music, film, radio, television) and its musical styles, such as Library Music, Easy Listening, Traditional Pop, and early Psychedelia, filtered through Ambient techniques. The production techniques for hauntology feature studio effects such as "decaying" (vinyl noise, tape hiss, etc.), reverb, echo, filtering, fidelity-shifting, time-warping, and other means to capture the "grain" of the recording. The instrumental palette for hauntology mainly consists of samplers, tape recorders, turntables, found sounds, and analog synthesizers. Hauntology shares overlap in style with Sound Collage and Plunderphonics through the shared use of sound manipulation and appropriation.

The term hauntology was coined by philosopher Jacques Derrida in his 1993 book Specters of Marx as a portmanteau of haunting and ontology. This term was later re-appropriated into a musical sense by UK cultural music writers Simon Reynolds, Mark Fisher and Adam Harper. The alternative term memoradelia was also proposed by Patrick McNally around the same time. Hauntology's stylistic origins are disputed, though 1990s Downtempo acts such as Portishead, Massive Attack, and Boards of Canada share many sonic elements with the genre. At the turn of the millennium, hauntology as a musical approach was most widely publicized in the United Kingdom. The most notable acts within this initial wave of hauntology were The Focus Group, Belbury Poly, The Advisory Circle, Philip Jeck, and Leyland Kirby, most notably under The Caretaker, whose later works within the Everywhere at the End of Time series revitalized interest in hauntology into the streaming age. UK-based labels such as Ghost Box and Trunk Records, inspired by The BBC Radiophonic Workshop, released works from many hauntology musicians. Despite association with the United Kingdom, particularly the cultural influence of British media, musicians outside the region have created hauntology works, such as the American William Basinski. Stylistic relatives to hauntology include Hypnagogic Pop, Chillwave, Vaporwave, and the multimedia concept of retrofuturism, all of which employ similar approaches and goals of warping and modernizing past styles and aesthetics.
