Audio Documentary

AKA: Radio Documentary • 317 releases • 1 cataloged
Form of documentary that uses audio to document or explore a subject or theme.
Read more Audio documentaries are a type of long-form, non-fiction recording that uses audio to document or explore a subject or theme. Audio documentaries typically use a mixture of narration, Interview, Field Recordings, music and Sound Effects to convey a narrative.

While the use of audio recording devices as a tool for preservation can be observed as far back as the 1800s, it wasn't until the 1930s that audio documentaries began to emerge as a distinct form, alongside the development and spread of radio as entertainment. The earliest radio documentaries were limited by the lack of portability of recording devices, and broadcast restrictions on the use of pre-recorded materials, instead relying on live interviews and dramatic re-enactment of events.

In the late 1930s, the easing of restrictions on pre-recorded materials following Herbert Morrison's eye witness report on the Hindenburg Disaster, and ban on simulated news reports following the broadcast of Orson Welles's The War of the Worlds: Invasion From Mars, allowed audio documentaries to become more actuality-based and to more closely resemble the contemporary audio documentary style. Further evolution of the format occurred in the 1940s with the development of commercial reel-to-reel tape recorders, allowing for increased portability and the ability to easily edit recordings.

While audio documentaries originated with radio, many audio documentarians have avoided this format to present their documentaries, instead using physical media, podcasts, or online streaming services to present their work. Sometimes this is due to the nature of their work. For example, Grant Wakefield released The Fire This Time on CD after his work was rejected for broadcast by several mainstream broadcasters.

Additionally, some musicians and sound artists have used the audio documentary format as an extension of their other creative works. For example, between 1967 and 1977 the Canadian classical pianist Glenn Gould created Glenn Gould's Solitude Trilogy: Three Sound Documentaries, a series of experimental broadcasts for the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation, incorporating ideas from Classical Music in its construction.
