Reggae / Ska / Dancehall

13,074 releases • 19 cataloged
Umbrella for related forms of Jamaican Music which have grown beyond their regional roots to become global phenomena.
Read more Reggae, Ska and Dancehall are related forms of Jamaican Music which have grown beyond their regional roots to become global phenomena. Characterized by a distinct off-beat "skank" rhythm and linked by an evolutionary history, this family of genres began with the development of Jamaican Ska in the early 1960s.

Influenced by American Rhythm & Blues and Jazz, as well as Caribbean genres such as Calypso and Mento, this lively and danceable style branched off into a slower, more soulful genre known as Rocksteady around 1966, which in turn led to the development of the more rhythm-focused reggae by 1968. In addition to its popularity in Jamaica, reggae also found early success in the United Kingdom through artists such as Desmond Dekker, Jimmy Cliff and The Upsetters. It would first reach a more widespread international audience with the 1972 film The Harder They Come, starring Cliff and featuring music by Cliff, Dekker, The Maytals and more. Later in the 70s, the genre would experience massive global popularity with the spread of the more spiritual and socially conscious Roots Reggae, shepherded by the iconic Bob Marley. Many artists also found success in the global market with crossover Pop Reggae hits in the 70s and beyond. At the end of the decade, ska also experienced a new wave of popularity with the emergence of 2 Tone in the United Kingdom.

Another key 1970s evolution in reggae would focus further on rhythm, with producers like Lee "Scratch" Perry and King Tubby developing Dub by experimenting with remixing techniques on reggae instrumentals. The unique, bass-heavy aesthetic of dub would prove highly influential to a wide range of other genres, such as Post-Punk, Hip Hop, and many Electronic Dance Music styles. Other artists would recycle rhythm tracks to be used in the burgeoning dancehall scene, which—in contrast with roots reggae—sought to reflect day-to-day life of the poor and working class by featuring simple, danceable songs and often vulgar lyrics. These reusable rhythm tracks, known as riddims, were also created electronically by Digital Dancehall pioneers such as King Jammy from the mid-1980s. By the 90s, fusions of dancehall with electronic riddims and rapping were globally popular. Meanwhile, ska underwent a third wave of success as Ska Punk emerged in the United States.

In the 21st century, dancehall reached new heights of popularity as it crossed over with mainstream Pop and Contemporary R&B worldwide. Today, the related styles of reggae, ska and dancehall are widely diverse, despite having shared underlying features and cultural lineage.

Hierarchy

See all genres
    • Reggae / Ska / Dancehall
      • Dancehall

        Developed from Reggae in the late 1970s, when Roots Reggae became more stripped-down, with an increased emphasis on pure rhythm.

        • Bubbling

          Originating in the Netherlands in the late 1980s, fusing sped-up Dancehall with Dutch Antillean music and contemporary Dutch rave.

        • Digital Dancehall

          First, pre-Ragga style of Dancehall to popularize Electronic rhythms that arose in the mid-1980s.

        • Flex Dance Music

          Largely instrumental; infused with Electronic Dance Music elements.

        • Gommance

          Emerged in Réunion, mixing Caribbean, African and Afro-Dutch influences.

        • Ragga

          Hardcore style with a deejay (equivalent to an MC in Hip Hop) rapping over predominantly Electronic rhythms.

        • Shatta

          French Caribbean Dancehall subgenre with a focus on sparse percussion, heavy basslines and lyrics revolving around partying and sensuality, often in Creole.

        • Trap Dancehall

          Combines midtempo Dancehall rhythms and tempos with major elements borrowed from Trap, namely in percussion and drum rhythms.

        • Zess

          Trinidadian style that originated in the country's slums in the late 2010s, defined by its sexual, violent, and occasionally gang-related lyrics and its influence from Soca, as well as occasional influences from Hip Hop and Funk brasileiro.

      • Reggae

        Developed in the 1960s; shuffling "bubble" and offbeat rhythms played on an organ, and staccato guitar and piano chords known as "skank."

        • Deejay

          Reggae in which a deejay (similar to an MC in Hip Hop) talks or chants lyrics over a pre-recorded reggae rhythm.

        • Digital Dancehall

          First, pre-Ragga style of Dancehall to popularize Electronic rhythms that arose in the mid-1980s.

        • Dub

          Primarily instrumental Reggae which developed from the extensive use of studio techniques and effects like reverb and delay.

        • Lovers Rock

          Smooth, quiet Reggae beats with lyrics focusing on romance and love.

        • Pacific Reggae

          Developed in Pacific island communities, incorporating elements from local Oceanian Music styles.

          • Jawaiian

            Hawaiian branch of Reggae, typically blends local traditional melodies, harmonies, incorporating specialty instruments such as ukulele and steel guitar.

        • Pop Reggae

          Light, commercial form of Reggae designed to crossover and break through to a more mainstream Pop audience.

        • Roots Reggae

          Primarily defined through the political, spiritual, and socially conscious messages of its lyrics.

          • Dub Poetry

            Poetry with Dub and Reggae backing rhythms, originating in Jamaica in the 1970s.

        • Seggae

          Fusion of Séga and Reggae, popular on Mauritius and other Mascarene Islands.

        • Skinhead Reggae

          Early subgenre prominent from 1968 to 1971; known for its high tempos and driving rhythms compared with the more languid pace of most later styles.

      • Rocksteady

        Jamaican popular music that blossomed briefly between 1966 and 1968 as an evolution of Ska, being slower, more relaxed, and soulful.

      • Ska

        Originated in Jamaica in the late 1950s, characterized by a signature off-beat rhythm (referred to as the ‘skank’ rhythm), chop-style guitar work, busy bass lines and a use of horn sections.

        • 2 Tone

          Combines 1960s Jamaican Ska with Punk Rock and New Wave to create tracks played at faster tempos and with a harder edge than the original Jamaican records.

        • Jamaican Ska

          Originated in Jamaica with groups who combined Jazz and Rhythm & Blues with rhythms of traditional Caribbean Music like Mento and Calypso.

        • New Tone

          Musically and socio-politically progressive movement noted for combatting prejudices and mixing together different styles of music, such as Electronic, Hip Hop, or Emo.

        • Spouge

          Fusion of Calypso and Ska originating in Barbados during the 1960s and 70s.

        • Third Wave Ska

          Expanded on the 2 Tone period, adding further Rock elements resulting in a heavier sound.

          • Ska Punk

            Utilizes skank rhythms, walking basslines, and often horn instrumentation in combination with a fast-paced Punk Rock playstyle.

            • Skacore

              Fuses skank rhythms and frantic blasts of horns with hardcore intensity.

              • Crack Rock Steady

                Incorporates elements of Crust Punk and sometimes Metal, coupled with anti-establishment lyrics.
