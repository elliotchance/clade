Bugle Call

18 releases
Short tunes played on a bugle; used in militaries for communication.
Read more Bugle calls are a form of musical communication traditionally used in the military, particularly in Western cultures. They are short, recognisable songs played on a bugle or similar instrument, intended to be piercing and clear in noisy conditions. Different tunes correspond to different orders and events, depending on the specific culture. As bugles lack valves, bugle calls are played using only a single harmonic scale, even when played on a more complex instrument such as a trumpet.

Perhaps the most well-known bugle calls are those used to mark funerals and official ceremonies of mourning, including "Taps" in America and "Last Post" in commonwealth nations. Bugle calls have influenced classical music, being for example used in Capriccio italien by Пётр Чайковский [Pyotr Tchaikovsky]. They have also influenced popular music, with a clear example being the well known jazz standard, the "Bugle Call Rag".

The use of calls on simple trumpets for military communication dates back to ancient times, with accounts of Romans using a bugle-like instrument known as a buccina. The use of bugle calls would be standard throughout Europe for centuries, influencing certain classical pieces. In America, Fife and Drum Corps would be the most prevalent form of military communication until the civil war, when the use of bugle calls were adopted. Today the practice is obsolete in practical use, but still widely used in ceremonies, media and historical reenactments, leaving them widely recognisable to the general public.
